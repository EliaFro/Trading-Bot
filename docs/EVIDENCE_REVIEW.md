# The Evidence on Trading Profitability: Who Actually Makes Money, and Whether Retail Day Trading Can Work

*External literature compilation, added 2026-07-22. Nothing below was produced
by this repository's harness — this is the field's evidence base, compiled
after our own studies concluded, and it sits alongside our independent
findings ([PHASE2_RESULTS.md](PHASE2_RESULTS.md),

---

## TL;DR

- **Directional short-horizon retail trading is structurally unprofitable after costs across every major market and era studied.** Complete-record academic datasets from Brazil, Taiwan, the US, India, and global crypto converge on roughly **1% of day traders earning reliable net profits**, with **70–97% losing money**. This repo's own finding — a ~1 basis point (bp) measured edge against a ~31 bp round-trip cost — is the identical result expressed in transaction-cost terms.
- **The reliably profitable participants are overwhelmingly "toll collectors" (market makers, exchanges, brokers) and scale/speed/infrastructure players**, not directional predictors. Virtu Financial disclosed **one losing day in 1,238** (spread capture, not forecasting), and Jane Street earned **$20.5bn net trading revenue in 2024** from market-making, not from betting on direction.
- **Most visible "profitable trader" income is sold, not traded.** Prop-firm challenge fees, courses, and signals form a multi-billion-dollar industry whose economics depend on participant failure; and with millions of participants, thousands of multi-year lucky track records are statistically guaranteed (survivorship + multiple-testing).

## Key Findings

1. **The base rates are damning and consistent.** Chague, De-Losso & Giovannetti (Brazil): 97% of persistent day traders lost money. Barber/Lee/Liu/Odean (Taiwan, complete records, 450,000+ traders): <1% predictably earn positive net returns. SEBI (India, ~9.6 million traders): 91% of F&O traders lost money in FY25. ESMA (EU CFDs): 74–89% of retail accounts lose. BIS (global crypto, 95 countries): ~three-quarters of Bitcoin app users lost money.
2. **The profitable minority in these datasets are quasi-professionals**, not hobbyists: high-volume, high-infrastructure traders, often earning effective fee advantages.
3. **Market makers/HFT make money from spread capture and rebates, not prediction** — the structural evidence (Virtu's near-perfect daily win record) reflects a toll-collection business model.
4. **1 basis point of edge is a viable business only at institutional scale/speed** and with maker rebates; at retail size against retail taker fees it is nothing.
5. **The visible exceptions are statistically indistinguishable from luck** given population size — the multiple-testing / deflated-Sharpe literature quantifies exactly how false winners arise.

## Details

### Section 1 — Retail day-trader outcome studies (the base rates)

These are the empirical bedrock. The strongest studies use **complete broker/exchange records** (not surveys), which eliminates self-report and survivorship bias.

|---|---|---|---|---|

Supporting datapoints (weaker methods, labeled): a 2025 survey of 1,005 first-year retail crypto traders found 84% lost money (survey, not complete records); the Colombian Stock Exchange (5.38m trades, 42,000 investors, 2006–2016) found retail negative abnormal returns of 4–4.4%/year before costs.

**Interpretation.** The striking feature is convergence across radically different markets, instruments, regulatory regimes, and decades, using the highest-quality data (complete records). The number that recurs — roughly 1% of day traders with durable net skill; 70–97% losing — is not an artifact of any one market. The Brazilian "no evidence of learning" result is particularly important: it refutes the "screen time makes you profitable" premise that the education industry sells.

### Section 2 — How the profitable ones actually make money (structural mechanisms)

**(a) Market making / HFT economics.** The canonical evidence is Virtu Financial's 2014 IPO S-1, which stated verbatim: *"As a result of our real-time risk management strategy and technology, we had only one losing trading day during the period depicted, a total of 1,238 trading days"* (Jan 1, 2009–Dec 31, 2013). Virtu's 2015 re-filing added that in 2014 it averaged ~5.3 million trades/day globally and *"profitably exited 49% of our overall positions."* Virtu describes itself as market-neutral, not taking speculative directional positions — it earns tiny spreads and rebates on enormous volume. This is the signature of a **toll-collection / spread-capture** business, not directional prediction: a market maker quotes both sides, captures the bid-ask spread and maker rebates, and manages inventory so the *portfolio* stays near-flat day to day. The near-perfect daily record is evidence *of* the mechanism (thousands of independent tiny edges → central-limit smoothing) — the fact that it profitably exits only ~49% of individual positions confirms it is not forecasting direction.

Scale of these businesses today: **Jane Street generated a record $20.5bn net trading revenue and $13bn net income in 2024** (nearly double 2023's $10.6bn), per Bloomberg — a haul that put it ahead of Bank of America ($18.8bn) and Citigroup ($19.8bn) that year; it then reported $39.6bn trading revenue in 2025 with only ~3,500 employees. Citadel Securities executes **approximately 35% of all US-listed retail volume** (per its own market-structure reporting; third-party estimates run 35–40%).

Industry profitability over time, however, shows spread capture is competed away. US-equities HFT profits **peaked near $5bn in 2009 (Rosenblatt Securities estimate) and fell to ~$1.25bn by 2012** — a ~74% decline (a figure attributed to Frederi Viens of Purdue and consistent with Rosenblatt/TABB estimates). Per-share HFT profit fell from ~$0.001–0.0015 to ~$0.0005–0.00075; HFT share volume fell from ~3.25bn to ~1.6bn shares/day. This decline itself proves the point: the edge is a structural/technological rent that erodes as competitors match speed, not a repeatable directional signal.

**(b) Payment for order flow (PFOF) & internalization.** Retail order flow is monetized by wholesalers who internalize it. In 2020–21, Citadel Securities paid ~$2.6bn in PFOF (most of it options); total PFOF across major brokers hit ~$2.5bn in 2020 (up from $892m in 2019) and a record ~$4.9bn (equities + options) more recently. Robinhood derived roughly half to three-quarters of its revenue from PFOF. The top three wholesalers (Citadel ~41%, Virtu ~26%, G1X ~16%) handled >80% of retail orders in 2022. Retail flow is prized precisely because it is "uninformed" — low adverse-selection risk — so the wholesaler reliably captures spread. (The SEC fined Citadel Securities $22.6m in 2017 for misleading broker clients about how it priced internalized retail orders.) The retail trader is the raw material in this chain, not a competitor within it.

**(c) Fee-tier asymmetry (crypto).** On Binance spot, base retail fees are 0.10% maker / 0.10% taker; at VIP 9 they fall to ~0.0110% maker / 0.0230% taker. In USD-M futures, base is 0.02% / 0.05%, dropping to **0.00% maker / 0.017% taker at VIP 9**. Designated market makers/qualified liquidity providers on many venues earn **negative maker fees (rebates)** — they are paid to trade. A retail taker paying ~0.10% per side (~0.20–0.31% round trip once spread/slippage is included) is competing against professionals who pay near-zero or are *paid*. This is the direct mechanism behind this repo's measured ~31 bp retail round-trip cost.

**(d) Scale economics of 1 bp.** 1 bp of edge on $100 of turnover is $0.01. On $10bn/day of turnover it is $1,000,000/day. A market maker with a ~1 bp average edge, near-zero or negative fees, and tens of billions in daily volume runs a large, low-variance business; a retail account with the same 1 bp edge but a 31 bp cost has a **–30 bp expectancy per trade** and is guaranteed to bleed out. Edge-per-trade is roughly constant across participants, but *cost-per-trade* is dramatically lower for professionals, and *total profit* scales with volume the professional can access and the retail trader cannot.

### Section 3 — The selling-the-dream industry

**(a) Prop-firm / funded-account challenge economics.** Independent data (FPFX Technology, 300,000+ accounts across 10 firms) show ~5–10% pass an evaluation on first attempt (FTMO cited ~9–10%; ~14% across all attempts), and **only ~7% of accounts ever reach a payout**. Average total payouts run ~4% of gross evaluation revenue. Evaluation fees average ~$4,270 per trader; the typical trader needs ~3 attempts (>$1,600 in fees for a $100k challenge). The model is a for-profit exam whose revenue depends on failure volume and repurchases. The prop-education/challenge market is estimated at ~$20bn with 2,000+ firms; FTMO alone posted $329m revenue in 2024 (+53% YoY). **Roughly 80–100 prop firms shut down in 2024** amid the MetaQuotes platform crackdown and regulatory pressure.

The landmark enforcement action: **CFTC v. Traders Global Group ("My Forex Funds"), filed August 2023** — alleging the firm, then the largest forex prop firm globally with 135,000+ customers, operated a simulated-trading platform that profited from trader losses, using execution manipulation (slippage, trade delays, "pretexts" to terminate profitable accounts) and generating **$310m+ in fees**. Importantly, in 2025 a Special Master recommended dismissal and sanctions against the CFTC for procedural misconduct (false statements to the court); the litigation collapsed, but the *description of the business model* — a house that profits when clients fail — is corroborated separately from the case's legal fate and is the structurally important point.

**(b) Education / guru economics.** The formal trading-education market is estimated at ~$1.5–2.1bn (2024–2026; commercial market-research estimates such as Verified Market Reports and Business Research Insights — low confidence, undisclosed methodology). Incentives are structurally misaligned: a genuinely repeatable edge is more valuable deployed than taught, so the marginal seller of "how to trade" content is selecting for people whose comparative advantage is marketing, not trading.

**(c) Signal-selling / copy-trading / "finfluencers."** The strongest academic evidence is Kakhbod, Kazempour, Livdan & Schürhoff, *Finfluencers* (2023 working paper; CEPR DP 20204, 2025), analyzing 29,000+ StockTwits finfluencers. Verbatim: *"28% of finfluencers are skilled, generating 2.6% monthly abnormal returns, 16% are unskilled, and 56% have negative skill ('antiskill') generating −2.3% monthly abnormal returns"* — and crucially, *"antiskilled finfluencers have more followers and more influence on retail trading than skilled finfluencers."* A contrarian strategy against them earned ~1.2%/month out-of-sample. The people most followed for advice are, on average, the ones whose advice loses money.

**(d) Survivorship + multiple-testing.** With millions of participants, extreme lucky streaks are statistically guaranteed. The formal literature: White's Reality Check (data-snooping bias); Bailey & López de Prado's *Deflated Sharpe Ratio* (JPM, 2014) and *Pseudo-Mathematics and Financial Charlatanism* (Notices of the AMS, 2014), which show that testing many strategy configurations inflates in-sample Sharpe into statistical mirages that collapse out-of-sample; and Harvey, Liu & Zhu, *…and the Cross-Section of Expected Returns* (Review of Financial Studies, 2016), which shows that after correcting for the hundreds of factors tested, a genuine discovery needs a **t-statistic > 3.0**, not the conventional 2.0, and concludes *"most claimed research findings in financial economics are likely false"* — only ~9 of 313 tested factors survive their >|3| criterion. The same math that flags a data-mined backtest flags a "5-year verified track record" drawn from a population of millions: both are selected ex post from a huge number of trials.

### Section 4 — The small true-skill population (honest treatment)

The literature does *not* claim skill is nonexistent — it claims it is rare, concentrated, and professional. In Taiwan, the persistent winners were the **top ~500 of ~450,000 (<1%)**, earning +37.9 bps/day net, and were high-volume quasi-professionals. Even among *heavy* day traders (>US$20k/day) only ~17–20% beat the market net of fees in a given year. These traders share: very high volume, professional infrastructure, and often effective fee advantages. Realistically they represent on the order of 1% (durably profitable) to at most 1–3% of active retail participants.

On whether retail-accessible anomalies survive costs and multiple-testing: McLean & Pontiff, *Does Academic Research Destroy Stock Return Predictability?* (Journal of Finance, 2016), find published anomaly returns decline **~26% out-of-sample and ~58% post-publication** (a ~32% publication/arbitrage effect on top of ~26% data-mining shrinkage), across 97 predictors. Long-horizon momentum/trend-following is the anomaly with the most robust survival record, but it operates at monthly-to-annual horizons with modest Sharpe — the opposite of intraday retail trading — and still degrades as it is crowded. There is no documented, transaction-cost-surviving, multiple-testing-robust *intraday* retail anomaly.

### Section 5 — Synthesis

The evidence forms a coherent picture. The reliably profitable participants in markets are:

- **(a) Toll collectors** — market makers (Virtu, Citadel Securities, Jane Street), exchanges (fee tiers, colocation), and brokers (PFOF, spreads, financing). They earn spread and rebates, not directional prediction, and their profit scales with *volume* and *fee advantage*.
- **(b) Scale / speed / infrastructure players** — HFT and quant firms whose edge is technological rent that erodes under competition (hence the 2009→2012 profit collapse from ~$5bn to ~$1.25bn), sustainable only at institutional scale.
- **(c) Sellers of services to aspirants** — prop-challenge operators, course sellers, signal/copy sellers — whose revenue depends on the *inflow* of hopeful losers, not on trading edge.

Directional short-horizon retail trading sits in none of these categories. Across Brazil, Taiwan, the US, India, the EU, and global crypto, using complete records, it is structurally unprofitable after costs, with a ~1% durable-winner tail who are effectively professionals. The visible exceptions are statistically indistinguishable from survivorship given the population size and the multiple-testing math.

**What this means for a retail account under $10k.** This project reached the correct conclusion by the most honest route — a real 3-year backtest. At retail crypto fee tiers, a ~31 bp round-trip cost against a ~1 bp edge is a structural **–30 bp expectancy per trade** that no amount of "discipline" or "screen time" overcomes — the Brazilian data's "no learning" finding confirms experience does not close this gap. For an account under $10k specifically: (1) you cannot access the VIP / maker-rebate fee tiers that make 1 bp a business, so the cost asymmetry is permanent; (2) you cannot achieve the volume over which tiny edges smooth out, so variance dominates and even a genuinely positive edge would take an infeasible sample to distinguish from luck; (3) the expected value of paying for a prop challenge, course, or signal service is negative given ~7% payout rates and 56% antiskilled finfluencers. The rational allocations are (a) low-cost, long-horizon, buy-and-hold index exposure — where you are the patient capital the toll-collectors serve, not the prey — and (b) if you want to deploy your quant skill, do so where the structural economics favor you (providing liquidity / earning rebates at sufficient scale, or in a professional/institutional seat), not paying retail taker fees to predict short-horizon direction.

## Caveats

- **Source quality varies and is flagged throughout.** Peer-reviewed, complete-record studies (Barber/Odean Taiwan & US; Chague et al. Brazil; SEBI; BIS; McLean & Pontiff; Harvey/Liu/Zhu; Bailey/López de Prado; Kakhbod et al.) are the strongest evidence. Prop-firm pass/payout statistics come largely from industry aggregators (FPFX, QuantVPS) and firm self-reports — directional, not independently audited. Trading-education market-size figures are commercial estimates with undisclosed methodology. HFT profit figures (Rosenblatt, TABB) are industry estimates, not audited accounts.
- **The My Forex Funds CFTC case was procedurally dismissed** with sanctions recommended against the regulator (2025); the *allegations* illustrate a business model but were not proven in court.
- **Survey-based crypto figures (e.g., the 84% first-year-loss survey) are weaker** than complete-record studies and are labeled as such. The BIS figures are careful back-of-envelope estimates from app-download and on-chain data, not individual account P&L.
- **"Skill exists but is rare" is the honest reading** — the evidence rejects "nobody can win," not just "most can't." The distinction matters: the winners are real, concentrated, professional, and represent <1–3% of participants.
- **Anomaly/factor evidence concerns longer horizons.** None of it rehabilitates intraday retail trading, and all of it degrades post-publication and under crowding.
- **Fee schedules and revenue figures are current as of 2024–2026** and change over time; VIP thresholds and exact loss-rate percentages (e.g., a given CFD broker's ESMA-mandated warning) should be re-verified against primary exchange/broker disclosures before quoting.
